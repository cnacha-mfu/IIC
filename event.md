# MFU's Inaugural International AI and Robotics Summit: Event Strategy and Design

**Mae Fah Luang University has a rare window to establish itself as the AI and robotics convening hub of the Greater Mekong Subregion by launching a flagship annual summit that no other institution in the region currently owns.** The Lancang-Mekong Cooperation framework's Joint Initiatives explicitly call for "exploring the possibility to establish a meeting mechanism for the LMC Innovation Corridor" — MFU's event can fill this institutional vacuum. With Thailand's Ministry of Higher Education (MHESI) designated as the national focal point for LMC Innovation Corridor Development, and the BOI having just approved its first Chinese humanoid robotics manufacturers for Thai production bases, the timing is optimal. This section outlines a complete event strategy — from concept and branding through logistics and expected deliverables — designed to position MFU as the permanent home of the GMS's premier AI and robotics gathering.

---

## Learning from the world's leading AI and robotics summits

The most successful model for MFU's ambitions combines elements from four proven event architectures. The **World Robot Conference (WRC)** in Beijing — now in its 10th year with **200,000+ on-site visitors, 200+ exhibitors, and 50,000 sqm of exhibition space** — demonstrates how a multi-component format (forums + expo + competitions + themed events) creates an ecosystem rather than a one-off gathering. WRC secured a congratulatory letter from President Xi Jinping at its inaugural 2015 edition and has since generated permanent infrastructure: Beijing's Embodied AI Robotics Innovation Center and the world's first commercial "robot 4S store." The lesson is clear — the event itself becomes a catalyst for institutional development.

The **World Artificial Intelligence Conference (WAIC)** in Shanghai offers the governance-plus-industry model MFU should emulate on a smaller scale. WAIC is co-organized by eight national-level bodies and has grown to **800+ exhibitors, 350,000+ visits, and 1,200+ guests from 30+ countries** across its eight editions. Critically for MFU, WAIC has maintained a dedicated **China-ASEAN AI Forum** for five consecutive years, demonstrating sustained appetite for cross-border AI cooperation in this exact corridor. WAIC's dual identity as both a technology showcase and a governance platform elevated Shanghai's AI sector output to **¥118 billion by Q1 2025**, up 29% year-over-year.

The most directly relevant precedent is the **China-ASEAN AI Application Ecosystem Exchange Conference** held in Nanning in September 2025 as part of the 22nd China-ASEAN Expo. With approximately **1,000 attendees**, it produced **50+ signed agreements, 30+ cross-border AI cooperation achievements**, launched the China-ASEAN AI Industry-University-Research Innovation Alliance, and unveiled platforms including the "Star Computing Cloud Pool" and a multilingual real-time translation system. The Nanning model proves that a single, well-designed event can simultaneously establish an alliance, launch platforms, and generate dozens of concrete partnerships — exactly the MFU playbook.

On the university-hosted side, the **NUS AI4X Conference** (inaugural 2025, ~600 attendees, 27 keynotes, 80 speakers including a Nobel laureate) and **Tsinghua's International AI Cooperation and Governance Forum** demonstrate how top universities leverage flagship events to position themselves as global thought leaders. Tsinghua's SIGS symposium model is particularly instructive: it combines Belt & Road framing with MOU signing ceremonies at every event, partnering with universities across Malaysia, Indonesia, and beyond alongside companies including Tencent and Midea. In Thailand itself, the **Techsauce Global Summit** has grown to **18,000+ attendees from 60+ countries** with extensive Thai government agency participation from DEPA, NSTDA, NIA, and TCEB — proving that Thai-hosted events can achieve world-class scale.

## Event concept: the Mekong AI Forum

### Naming and branding for longevity

The event name should anchor to geography, following the proven formula of iconic gatherings where the location becomes the brand: Davos, Boao, Shangri-La. The recommended name is the **"Mekong AI Forum"** (MAF), with the inaugural edition styled as the **"1st Mekong AI Forum: Intelligent Connections Across the Lancang-Mekong Corridor."** This name achieves several strategic objectives simultaneously. It claims the entire Mekong corridor as the event's intellectual territory, not just one university or city. It invokes the Lancang-Mekong Cooperation framework without being bureaucratically named. It is short, memorable, and pronounceable across Thai, Chinese, and English — critical for cross-border branding. And it signals permanence: "Forum" implies an ongoing institution, not a one-time conference. Alternative constructions like "Chiang Rai AI Summit" or "Golden Triangle Innovation Forum" were considered but rejected — the former is too locally bounded, and the latter carries associations with the region's narcotics history.

### Three-pillar format

The Mekong AI Forum should integrate three distinct but interconnected pillars under a single brand:

**Pillar 1 — The Summit** (International Conference on AI and Robotics for the GMS): Keynote addresses, plenary panels, and thematic sub-forums featuring academic researchers, government officials, and industry leaders. This is the intellectual anchor that justifies institutional and government participation.

**Pillar 2 — The Expo** (Chinese AI and Robotics Technology Showcase): A curated exhibition featuring live demonstrations from Chinese companies organized into thematic zones. This is the visual and experiential anchor that drives media coverage and public engagement.

**Pillar 3 — The Connect** (Industry-University Matchmaking and MOU Signing Forum): Pre-scheduled B2B and B2U meetings culminating in a formal MOU signing ceremony. This is the outcomes anchor that produces the tangible partnerships justifying the event's existence.

### Recommended 2.5-day structure

Drawing on the Robotics Summit & Expo (Boston) two-day model and the Automation World (Seoul) three-day model, the optimal structure for an inaugural event with **300–500 attendees** is:

**Day 0 (Half-day/Evening):** VIP arrival reception, welcome dinner at a Chiang Rai riverside venue, and informal networking for Chinese delegation leaders, Thai government officials, and MFU leadership. This intimate setting enables relationship-building before the formal program and follows Chinese business protocol expectations for pre-event hospitality.

**Day 1 — "Summit & Showcase":** Grand opening ceremony at 9:00 AM with a dramatic technology reveal (a UBTECH Walker humanoid or Unitree quadruped robot delivering the program book to MFU's president on stage — this becomes the viral media moment). Ribbon-cutting for the Technology Expo follows. The morning features three **20-minute keynote addresses** from a senior Chinese AI academician, a Thai government minister (MHESI or DEPA), and a leading Chinese robotics CEO. The afternoon runs **parallel thematic sub-forums**: AI for Agriculture (featuring XAG), Service Robotics for Hospitality and Healthcare (KEENON, Pudu, Fourier Intelligence), Computer Vision and NLP Applications (SenseTime, iFlytek), and Humanoid/Quadruped Robotics Frontiers (UBTECH, Unitree, SIASUN). The expo floor remains open continuously during all breaks and lunch. The day concludes with a gala dinner and, if budget allows, a **drone light show** over MFU's campus by XAG or DJI — an extraordinary visual for social media and news coverage.

**Day 2 — "Connect & Commit":** The morning opens with a keynote block on industry-university collaboration models, followed by **three hours of structured B2B/B2U matchmaking** using pre-scheduled 20-minute meeting rounds powered by a dedicated matchmaking platform (Grip, b2match, or Brella, activated 3–4 weeks before the event). After a working lunch, thematic roundtables address specific collaboration tracks: joint research labs, talent development pipelines, technology transfer frameworks, and student exchange programs. The pinnacle is the **formal MOU Signing Ceremony at 14:30** — a staged, media-ready event with institutional flags, professional lighting, ceremonial document exchange, and handshake photography. This is immediately followed by the **official launch of MFU's AI Innovation Center**, with the signing partners announced as founding collaborators. The closing ceremony at 15:30 announces concrete outcomes (number of MOUs signed, partnerships formed) and formally announces the dates for the 2nd Mekong AI Forum, signaling permanence from Day 1.

## Technology expo design: bringing Chinese robotics to the GMS

### Five thematic demonstration zones

Rather than organizing booths alphabetically, the expo should be structured into immersive thematic zones that tell a narrative about AI and robotics applications relevant to the GMS:

**"Sky Zone" — Aerial Intelligence:** XAG (P150 Max agricultural drone with 80kg payload, R150/R200 agricultural rovers, RealTerra precision mapping) and DJI (T-series agricultural drones, enterprise inspection drones, DJI Dock autonomous stations). MFU's campus and surrounding agricultural landscape offer the rare opportunity for **live outdoor drone demonstrations** — a powerful differentiator over urban conference venues. A netted indoor flying cage (minimum 10m × 10m × 5m) can supplement for weather contingencies.

**"Humanoid Avenue" — The Future of Embodied AI:** UBTECH (Walker S2 full-size humanoid, Tien Kung research humanoid at ~$43,000, Walker C compact tour guide), Unitree (G1 humanoid, Go2 quadruped robot dog), Fourier Intelligence (GR-1 humanoid at 165cm/55kg, AI exoskeleton rehabilitation systems deployed in 2,000+ hospitals across 40+ countries), and SIASUN (SE01 humanoid). Each company gets a minimum **5m × 5m clear demonstration area** with safety barriers for live walking, manipulation, and interaction demos.

**"Service Boulevard" — Robots in Daily Life:** KEENON (T-series delivery robots with **48.6% market share** in Chinese food service, C-series cleaning robots) and Pudu (BellaBot cat-face delivery robot, KettyBot). Live food and beverage delivery demonstrations through the expo hall make the technology immediately tangible and photogenic.

**"Industrial Intelligence Hub" — Smart Manufacturing:** SIASUN industrial robotic arms and collaborative robots for manufacturing simulation demonstrations. This zone connects to BOI-promoted sectors and Thailand 4.0's advanced automation priorities.

**"AI Brain Center" — Software and Intelligence Platforms:** iFlytek (real-time Mandarin-Thai-English translation demonstration, voice recognition, AI education tools) and SenseTime (computer vision, facial recognition, smart city visualization). Interactive screens and hands-on stations invite engagement.

A sixth **"Experience Zone"** should allow attendees to operate robots, try exoskeletons, control drone simulators, and interact directly with the technology — this is where social media content is generated organically.

### Technical requirements

The expo area requires a minimum of **2,000–3,000 sqm**, with **220V/30A+ dedicated circuits** for robot charging stations, **enterprise-grade WiFi** (minimum 100 Mbps dedicated) plus wired Ethernet at each booth, **wide loading access** for heavy equipment transport, and **customs clearance coordination** for equipment shipments from China. Adequate air conditioning is critical — both for attendee comfort in Chiang Rai's climate and for protecting sensitive electronics. Insurance, safety barriers for live robot demonstrations, and bilingual technical support staff are essential.

## Industry-university matchmaking that produces real partnerships

The matchmaking component is where the Mekong AI Forum must diverge most sharply from standard academic conferences. The goal is not networking — it is **pre-arranged, outcome-oriented meetings that lead to signed MOUs**.

### Pre-event preparation is the decisive factor

Best practice from events managing thousands of scheduled meetings (MIP Cancún recorded **2,418 pre-scheduled meetings**) shows that matchmaking success depends almost entirely on what happens before the event. All participants must create detailed profiles on the matchmaking platform specifying their collaboration interests, capabilities, and partnership objectives **3–4 weeks before the event**. MFU's organizing team should then curate match lists, proactively connecting Chinese companies with specific MFU faculties and research groups whose work aligns. Critically, **draft MOU templates should be circulated and pre-negotiated** with interested parties before the event so that the on-site signing ceremony is ceremonial rather than substantive negotiation.

### Types of partnerships to target

The matchmaking forum should pursue seven categories of agreements: joint research collaboration agreements (co-supervised graduate students, shared publications), talent development MOUs (internship pipelines, industry-sponsored scholarships, joint curriculum development), **joint laboratory establishment** (the highest-value outcome — e.g., "MFU-XAG Agricultural Drone Research Lab"), technology transfer frameworks, student and faculty exchange programs, equipment donation or loan agreements (companies provide cutting-edge hardware for teaching and research), and incubator partnerships (company mentors embedded in MFU's innovation ecosystem). Based on precedents from comparable inaugural events, **5–10 signed MOUs** is a realistic and ambitious target, with 2–3 being high-profile joint lab or strategic partnership agreements.

### MOU signing ceremony design

The ceremony itself must be designed for maximum media impact. A long signing table with institutional flags and a professional backdrop displaying the Mekong AI Forum logo and all partner logos. Two copies of each MOU in ceremonial folders. Brief welcoming remarks by MFU's president, introduction of each signing pair, simultaneous signing, document exchange, handshake photography, and a group photo of all signatories together. A designated media pit with professional lighting, a large LED screen showing close-ups for audience visibility, and a dedicated livestream camera ensure the ceremony produces headline-worthy content. Press releases with photographs should be distributed within one hour of the ceremony's conclusion.

## Strategic positioning: why Chiang Rai, why now

### The Lancang-Mekong corridor advantage

MFU already self-describes as **"the gateway to the Greater Mekong Sub-region, in the heart of the ASEAN region"** — the Mekong AI Forum operationalizes this claim. Chiang Rai sits at a unique nexus: three border districts connect directly to Myanmar (Mae Sai), Laos (Chiang Khong via the 4th Thai-Lao Friendship Bridge), and China's Yunnan province via the **R3A Highway** — approximately 1,200 km to Kunming through the GMS North-South Economic Corridor's Western Subcorridor. The Thai government has approved an intermodal transport facility at Chiang Khong specifically to serve as a cross-border trade gateway. Chiang Rai province's own strategic plan envisions a three-stage development from "Connecting City" to "Destination City" to **"GMS Economic Hub."** An AI innovation center anchored by an annual international forum advances this vision from aspiration to institution.

The geographic narrative is reinforced by existing infrastructure: **direct flights connect Chiang Rai's Mae Fah Luang International Airport to Kunming**, Mekong River shipping links Chiang Saen port to China's Guanlei port, and the China-Laos Railway is creating new connectivity pathways. Chiang Rai's three designated Special Economic Zones (Mae Sai, Chiang Saen, Chiang Khong) and active feasibility studies for a **cross-border SEZ under the LMC framework** linking Thailand, Myanmar, Laos, and China provide additional institutional scaffolding.

### Invoking the LMC Innovation Corridor framework

The Lancang-Mekong Cooperation framework offers MFU a powerful legitimizing superstructure. The **Joint Initiatives on LMC Innovation Corridor Development** (2023–2024) specify 10 priority areas, at least three of which directly mandate the kind of event MFU proposes: "Deepen STI digital cooperation," "Promote cooperation in innovation, entrepreneurship, and start-up ecosystem," and most critically, **"Explore establishing a meeting mechanism for the LMC Innovation Corridor."** The Mekong AI Forum can position itself as the answer to this explicit call — becoming the permanent meeting mechanism for AI and digital innovation under the LMC umbrella.

The **LMC Five-Year Plan of Action (2023–2027)** further supports this positioning by calling for cooperation frameworks for scientific and technical innovation, exploring innovation corridors in border areas and SEZs, strengthening digital skills capacity-building, and promoting STI cooperation platforms. The **Lancang-Mekong Cooperation Special Fund** — which has supported over 800 projects across the six member countries with typical grants of **$250,000–$460,000** — could potentially co-fund preparatory activities, capacity-building components, or follow-up research programs. The Mekong Institute, based in Khon Kaen, already hosted a high-level consultation on Lancang-Mekong Digital Innovation in Kunming in December 2024 with participation from 50 senior officials across approximately 40 agencies — a ready-made partner network for MFU to engage.

### Securing Thai government endorsement

Five Thai government agencies should be targeted as endorsers and active participants, each with a distinct value proposition:

**MHESI** (Ministry of Higher Education, Science, Research and Innovation) is the natural lead champion. As Thailand's designated focal point for LMC Innovation Corridor Development, MHESI's endorsement connects the event directly to the LMC framework. MHESI has also launched a dedicated **"MHESI for AI" policy** with three pillars: AI for lifelong learning, AI workforce development (targeting 30,000 professionals), and AI innovation applications — the Mekong AI Forum advances all three.

**BOI** (Board of Investment) brings investment credibility. BOI recorded a record 3,370 projects worth **฿1.876 trillion** in 2025, with AI, robotics, and automation among its top 2026 promotion priorities. BOI has approved its **first-ever humanoid robot components production base** with five Chinese tech companies including SIASUN, and its Shanghai roadshow attracted 600+ Chinese companies. BOI's participation signals to Chinese companies that Thailand is actively courting their investment.

**DEPA** (Digital Economy Promotion Agency) offers programmatic and financial support through its **Digital Event Marketing Fund**, Smart City development programs, and the recently established **Thailand AI Research Institute** (a joint venture with VISAI). DEPA operates regional offices including in Chiang Mai, providing proximate support infrastructure.

**NSTDA** brings research credibility through its Innovation and Technology Assistance Program (ITAP) network of 1,300+ university-based experts, its R&D tax incentive certification authority (200% deduction), and its track record running Thailand Tech Show and NSTDA Investors' Day events.

**TCEB** (Thailand Convention and Exhibition Bureau) can provide direct funding support for international conferences hosted in Thailand, along with promotional assistance and logistics coordination.

### Formally launching the AI Innovation Center

The MOU signing ceremony should culminate in the **official launch of MFU's AI Innovation Center**, with the signing partners announced as founding collaborators. This follows a proven model: the University of the West Indies launched its AI Innovation Centre in December 2025 with a combined opening ceremony, AI hackathon, and conference — it now houses 50+ members and 35+ projects with 20+ institutional partners. MFU should announce the Center's founding research agenda, its initial corporate and institutional partners, and its first funded projects (ideally including at least one LMC Special Fund or NSTDA-supported initiative). The narrative arc of the entire event should build toward this moment: from understanding the technology landscape (Summit), to experiencing the technology firsthand (Expo), to committing to partnerships (Connect), to launching the permanent institution that will steward these partnerships year-round (AI Innovation Center launch).

## Practical logistics and budget framework

### Budget framework: $150,000–$200,000 for a world-class inaugural event

Thailand's significant cost advantage — **40–60% lower** than comparable Western or Singapore-based events — means a high-impact inaugural summit is achievable within a **$150,000–$200,000 USD (approximately ฿5–7 million)** budget. MFU's in-kind contribution of venue, campus WiFi, staff support, and dining facilities substantially reduces the cash outlay.

| Category | Estimated Share | Estimated Range (USD) |
|---|---|---|
| Venue, setup, and signage | 15–20% | $22,000–$40,000 |
| AV, tech, livestreaming, WiFi upgrades | 10–15% | $15,000–$30,000 |
| Speaker travel and honoraria (10–15 speakers) | 15–20% | $22,000–$40,000 |
| Exhibition and expo infrastructure | 10–15% | $15,000–$30,000 |

| Simultaneous interpretation (Thai-Chinese-English) | 3–5% | $5,000–$10,000 |
| Logistics, transportation, and shuttles | 5–8% | $8,000–$15,000 |
| Staff and event management | 5–8% | $8,000–$15,000 |
| Contingency (10%) | 10% | $15,000–$20,000 |

The recommended funding mix: **university budget (20–25%)**, **government grants from TCEB, MHESI, and DEPA (25–30%)**, **corporate sponsorships in platinum/gold/silver tiers (30–35%)**, and **registration fees (10–15%)** at $100–200 for academics and $300–500 for industry participants. Chinese companies exhibiting their technology may accept booth fees or provide sponsorship in exchange for premium exhibition placement and speaking slots. Yunnan provincial government or CCPIT Yunnan may contribute funding for delegation logistics.

### Planning timeline: 14–16 months from initiation to event day

An inaugural event with Chinese corporate delegations requires longer lead times than standard academic conferences due to hierarchical Chinese corporate approval processes and the need to build institutional relationships from scratch.

### Attracting high-level Chinese speakers and delegations

Five channels should be activated in parallel. **Formal invitation letters from MFU's president** — Chinese business culture demands that invitations originate from the highest institutional authority, addressed to the CEO or chairman by name. **CCPIT Yunnan** (the China Council for the Promotion of International Trade's Kunming office), which regularly organizes outbound business delegations to Southeast Asia and has the geographic and economic corridor logic to support this event. **CAAI** (Chinese Association for Artificial Intelligence) as academic co-organizer or endorser, lending credibility to attract both researchers and the companies they collaborate with. **The Chinese Embassy in Bangkok and Consulate General in Chiang Mai**, whose Economic and Commercial sections can facilitate diplomatic channel invitations. And MFU's own **Confucius Institute and Sirindhorn Chinese Language and Culture Center** — a gift from the Chinese government, built in traditional Chinese garden style on campus — which provides an existing diplomatic and cultural bridge.

MFU should leverage concrete value propositions beyond prestige: market access to Southeast Asia for technology demonstrations, a **student talent pipeline** from MFU's 15,000-student body including the School of Applied Digital Technology, R&D collaboration in agriculture and healthcare applications uniquely suited to the GMS context, and the opportunity to be founding partners of a permanent AI Innovation Center at the gateway to ASEAN's fastest-growing corridor.

### MFU campus and Chiang Rai venue considerations

MFU's **800+ hectare campus** with modern facilities, full WiFi coverage, and a mountain setting offers a distinctive conference environment that urban venues cannot match. The university confirms its auditoria and conference halls are "fully equipped with modern audiovisual facilities" and regularly hosts seminars, symposia, and international conferences. The **Sirindhorn Chinese Language and Culture Center** could serve as a symbolic venue for the welcome reception or a VIP meeting room. For overflow capacity, the **Le Méridien Chiang Rai Resort** (26,460 sq ft of event space, the largest in Chiang Rai) and **The Riverie by Katathani** (5-star, capacity for 700 guests) are located 9–12 miles from campus. Mae Fah Luang International Airport is approximately **14 km from campus** (~20 minutes by car), with direct flights to Bangkok, Kunming, and Hong Kong. Dedicated shuttle buses between the airport, hotels, and campus should be arranged for all delegates.

## Expected outcomes and the path from event to institution

### Tangible deliverables from the inaugural Mekong AI Forum

The event should target specific, measurable outcomes that justify its investment and build momentum for Year 2:

- **5–10 signed MOUs** spanning joint research labs (the highest-value category), talent development pipelines, technology transfer frameworks, and student exchange agreements — with 2–3 being "headline" partnerships such as an "MFU-XAG Agricultural Drone Research Lab" or an "MFU-UBTECH Humanoid Robotics Education Center"
- **300–500 in-person attendees** with at least 30% international participation
- **20–50 media mentions** across Thai, Chinese, and international technology media outlets, with social media reach of 50,000–200,000 impressions
- **A joint declaration or communiqué** on AI and Robotics Cooperation for the Greater Mekong Subregion, co-signed by participating institutions
- **Published proceedings** (via Springer or similar academic publisher, following the CAAI/CICAI model) to create lasting academic value
- **Official launch of MFU's AI Innovation Center** with announced founding partners and initial research agenda

### From annual event to permanent institution

The Mekong AI Forum's true strategic value lies not in any single edition but in the institutional flywheel it creates. Within 30 days post-event, all MOU commitments should have designated implementation leads and quarterly review schedules. Within 3–6 months, joint research proposals should be submitted to bilateral funding mechanisms (NSTDA, China's NSFC, LMC Special Fund, e-ASIA Joint Research Program). Within 12 months, the first student exchange placements and visiting researcher arrivals should materialize. Based on precedents from comparable events, realistic 12–18 month follow-through includes **3–5 joint research proposals submitted, 10–20 student exchange placements, 1–3 technology transfer agreements, 2–4 industry-sponsored research projects, and 5–10 co-authored publications**.

The AI Innovation Center should be populated with the partnerships forged at the Forum: joint research labs equipped with partner-donated technology, visiting researchers from Chinese institutions, industry-sponsored graduate scholarships, and a startup incubation track with company mentors. The Forum becomes the Center's annual showcase, where progress is reported, new partnerships are launched, and the next year's research agenda is shaped. Over successive editions, this creates what the MIT Media Lab model demonstrates at scale: a **membership ecosystem** where corporate partners invest continuously because the research portfolio and talent pipeline no single company could build alone become available through sustained participation.

The ultimate strategic outcome is positional: MFU becomes synonymous with AI and robotics innovation in the Mekong corridor, the way Boao became synonymous with Asian economic dialogue and Davos with global governance. The university's geographic position — Thailand's northernmost research university, at the literal junction of the Lancang-Mekong corridor, housing a Chinese government-gifted cultural center — is an asset no competitor can replicate. The Mekong AI Forum transforms this geographic fact into an institutional identity.

## Conclusion: building the GMS's permanent AI convening platform

Three strategic insights should guide execution. First, the LMC Innovation Corridor's explicit call for a meeting mechanism creates a **policy-level mandate** that MFU can claim — this is not just a university event but a response to an intergovernmental directive, and it should be framed as such in all communications with MHESI and the LMC National Coordination Units. Second, the event must produce outcomes, not just conversations: pre-negotiated MOUs, a formal AI Innovation Center launch, and a published declaration transform the Forum from a conference into a **commitment device** that binds partners to action. Third, the inaugural edition's success should be measured not by scale but by **institutional permanence** — securing the government endorsements, corporate founding partners, and second-edition commitments that ensure the Mekong AI Forum becomes a fixture on the Asian innovation calendar. If MFU begins organizing committee formation and CCPIT engagement immediately, a **Q4 2027 or Q1 2028 inaugural edition** is achievable, allowing the 14–16 months needed to build the Chinese delegation relationships and government endorsements that will make the Forum's first edition feel not like a beginning, but like something that was always inevitable.
